Title: The Last Letter of Mrs. Halverson
Date: March 2025
Content: She had kept every letter she ever received — a hundred and twelve of them, filed by year in a hatbox that smelled of cedar and the nineteen-sixties. When she finally sat down to read them again, she discovered she had forgotten the names of almost all the senders.

The handwriting, however, she remembered perfectly. The looping g's of her college roommate, the aggressive capitals of her first employer, the almost hieroglyphic shorthand her mother used when she was in a hurry. The names had dissolved; the hands remained.

She began to write back to all of them. It didn't matter that most were dead, or moved, or simply beyond locating. What mattered was the weight of the pen, and the knowledge that some words are meant to be sent rather than kept.

Title: Everything That Fits in a Coat Pocket
Date: January 2025
Content: My grandfather could fit his entire life into a coat pocket. Or so he claimed. He meant it philosophically — he was that sort of man — but I spent most of my childhood testing the proposition literally.

A folded photograph of my grandmother at nineteen, before she was anyone's grandmother. A comb with two teeth missing, which he claimed improved airflow. A stub of pencil he had carried since 1971. A small stone from a beach whose name he refused to give me, saying only that it was a place one should find for oneself.

I have the coat now. The pockets are empty. But I've started to understand what he meant: it's not about what you carry, but about knowing what is worth the weight.

Title: Cartography of a Rented Room
Date: November 2024
Content: I have lived in eleven rented rooms. I remember each of them by a single detail: the radiator that ticked in Morse code, the window that opened only inward, the landlord's cat that sat at the top of the stairs like a toll collector.

The room I think about most is the one I lived in for only three weeks, between one life and the next. It had no distinguishing features at all. Magnolia walls, a single overhead light, a wardrobe that smelled of someone else's choices.

It was in that room — perhaps because of its perfect blankness — that I wrote the first story I was not ashamed of. Emptiness, I learned, is not the absence of a container. It is the container itself.

Title: A Short History of Pauses
Date: September 2024
Content: There is a pause my mother makes before she says something true. It lasts approximately two seconds, though it seems to expand the way certain rooms do when the furniture is removed.

I have inherited this pause. I notice it now in my own speech — that small hesitation, that breath held like a coin before a decision. People who don't know me well think I am uncertain. People who know me well know I am about to say the only thing that matters.

My mother says she got it from her mother, who got it from hers. A matrilineal inheritance of silence. I wonder sometimes what would happen if we all paused at the same moment — all the women in that long chain of pauses, holding breath across the decades.

Perhaps we already do.
